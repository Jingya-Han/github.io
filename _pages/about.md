Hi! I am Jingya Han. I am now a PhD candidate in Atmospheric Sciences at Cornell University. I am interested in studying precipitation.

My journey into precipitation research began with a moment of curiosity during my undergraduate studies at Northeast Normal University, when a course on global environmental change first exposed me to the profound societal impacts of extreme rainfall. Motivated by both scientific intrigue and real-world consequences, I initiated my first research project as an undergraduate, leading a team to investigate changes in extreme precipitation across dry and wet regions of China. Through this experience, I developed my foundation in data analysis, coding, and scientific writing, ultimately publishing my first first-author paper—an experience that solidified my commitment to climate research.

During my master’s training at Beijing Normal University, my work expanded from analysis to data construction. I independently led the development of a high-resolution gridded daily precipitation dataset from gauge observations across China, overcoming challenges in data homogenization, interpolation, and large-scale computation. This experience marked a transition from a guided learner to an independent researcher capable of designing end-to-end scientific workflows.

Now, as a PhD at Cornell University, my research focuses on understanding how precipitation intensity distributions respond to climate change, with an emphasis on reconciling and diagnosing discrepancies between station observations and climate model simulations. By combining statistical diagnostics, large observational datasets, and climate model outputs, I aim to uncover the physical mechanisms driving changes in precipitation extremes and reduce uncertainties in future projections. Across all stages of my academic journey, a common thread has been a desire to connect fundamental climate processes with their real-world impacts, and to contribute to a more predictive understanding of the hydrological cycle in a warming climate.

## Research Interests

- Precipitation on large scales
- Extreme climate events detection, attribution, and projection
- Aerosol forcing
